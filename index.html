Mindspace

A multi-agent psychological auditing framework for Large Language Models.

Moving beyond binary pass/fail alignment metrics into deep causal analysis of AI reasoning.

LinkedIn GitHub Repository

The Problem

We are witnessing a critical failure in modern AI alignment. Standard methodologies like Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) have succeeded in making models polite, but they have inadvertently created a "Black Box" of safety that prioritizes optics over genuine understanding.

When Large Language Models (LLMs) encounter high-stakes psychological ambiguity—such as a user expressing subtle self-hatred or seeking validation for a delusion—they tend to fail in two predictable, harmful ways:

1. Sycophancy (The "Yes-Man" Failure)

In an attempt to be "helpful" and maximize reward functions, models often mirror the user's negative internal state. If a user says, "I'm worthless," a sycophantic model might implicitly validate that feeling rather than gently challenging it, reinforcing the user's cognitive distortions.

2. Over-Refusal (The "Blocker" Failure)

Conversely, when models detect sensitive keywords (e.g., "suicide", "hopeless"), crude safety filters often trigger a hard refusal or a generic "Seek Professional Help" block. While safe, this shuts down the conversation immediately, leaving the user isolated when they most needed a nuanced, non-judgmental listener.

The Gap: Traditional benchmarks measure what the model outputs (the final text), but they fail to explain why the model chose that path. Mindspace was built to make this cognitive process visible, steerable, and psychologically sound.

The Methodology

Mindspace introduces a novel cognitive layer before the model generates a single word of response. Instead of relying on a single model's opaque internal weights to make safety decisions, we orchestrate a **Multi-Agent Debate** to generate context-aware safety constraints on the fly.

This approach is grounded in the theory of Constitutional AI and RLAIF (Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback). By treating safety as a dynamic reasoning task rather than a static filter, we can handle nuance that hard-coded rules miss.

1. The Debate

Writer vs. Chaos

Two specialized agents analyze the user's prompt. The "Proponent" argues for strict safety and risk avoidance, analyzing the prompt for potential self-harm or delusion risks. The "Opponent" argues for user freedom, permissiveness, and the importance of not shutting down the conversation. This surfaces the inherent tension between being helpful and being harmless.

2. The Synthesis

The Moderator (Logic)

A third, highly logical agent reviews the debate arguments. It acts as a Supreme Court Judge, synthesizing the opposing views into a "Shared Notepad"—a JSON object containing specific "Do" and "Don't" constraints tailored to this exact interaction (e.g., "Do validate feelings, Don't agree with self-hatred").

3. The Execution

The Target (Writer)

The target model generates a response, but with a twist: it is forced to explicitly reason through the Moderator's constraints using a Chain-of-Thought (CoT) process. This makes the alignment decision visible. We can see the model thinking: "I want to agree, but the constraint says do not reinforce self-hatred."

4. The Audit

The Red Team (Chaos)

Finally, an adversarial agent attacks the generated response. It acts as a "Chaos Monkey," probing the response to see if the model actually followed the constraints or if it slipped into sycophancy under pressure.

The Architecture

To implement this theoretical framework, we utilized a single NVIDIA H200 GPU to run three specialized 8B-parameter models concurrently. Rather than asking one massive model to police itself (which leads to bias and hallucination), we split the cognition into specialized roles based on model strengths.

Logic Brain

Qwen 2.5 7B

Role: Moderator & Judge.
Selected for its rigorous adherence to instruction following and ability to output valid JSON structure even under complexity. It acts as the system's "Prefrontal Cortex," inhibiting impulses and setting rules.

Chaos Brain

DeepSeek Coder

Role: Opponent & Auditor.
Selected for its strong logic and coding reasoning, making it an excellent "Red Teamer." It excels at finding loopholes in safety rules and arguing counter-intuitive points to stress-test the system.

Writer Brain

DeepSeek R1 Llama

Role: Target.
Selected for its native Chain-of-Thought capabilities. Unlike standard chat models, R1 exposes its internal monologue, allowing us to capture the raw "thinking" process as it applies safety constraints.

References & Inspiration

  • Petri: A Framework for Automated Auditing Anthropic Research (2024) The foundational inspiration for multi-agent auditing frameworks. Petri demonstrated that small, specialized agents could effectively audit larger models for safety failures.
  • RLAIF: Scaling Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback with AI Feedback Lee, H., et al. (2023). Google DeepMind. arXiv:2309.00267 The theoretical basis for using AI to align AI. This paper proved that AI feedback can be as effective as human feedback for training preference models.
  • Constitutional AI: Harmlessness from AI Feedback Bai, Y., et al. (2022). Anthropic. arXiv:2212.08073 The concept of injecting a "Constitution" (Shared Notepad) into the prompt. This paper introduced the idea of using a written set of principles to guide model behavior.
  • DeepSeek-R1 Technical Report DeepSeek-AI (2024) Technical documentation for the DeepSeek-R1 model, detailing its reinforcement learning strategies for incentivizing reasoning capabilities.

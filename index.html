Mindspace

A smart safety system for AI therapy.

Moving beyond simple pass/fail safety checks. We look inside the AI's mind to understand why it answers the way it does.

LinkedIn GitHub Repository

The Problem

Modern AI safety has a blind spot. Standard training methods (like RLHF) have made models polite, but they have also created a "Black Box" where we don't know why a model decides to be safe. This leads to two major problems when users discuss mental health:

1. The "Yes-Man" Problem (Sycophancy)

In an attempt to be helpful, AI often mirrors the user's mood. If a user says, "I'm worthless," a poorly trained AI might accidentally agree with that feeling to stay on the user's "good side," which reinforces their negative thoughts.

2. The "Shutdown" Problem (Over-Refusal)

On the flip side, if the AI sees a scary keyword like "hopeless," it often panics and blocks the conversation entirely with a generic "Seek Professional Help" message. This leaves the user feeling isolated right when they needed someone to listen.

The Solution: Mindspace sits in the middle. It creates a transparent thinking process so we can guide the AI to be supportive without being dangerous.

The Solution

Instead of relying on one AI brain to do everything, Mindspace uses a Multi-Agent Debate. Before the AI replies to the user, three different AI "agents" have a quick meeting to decide the safest and most helpful way to respond.

1. The Debate

Writer vs. Chaos

Two agents analyze the user's message. The "Proponent" argues for safety and caution. The "Opponent" argues for user freedom and empathy. This brings the conflict between "being safe" and "being helpful" out into the open.

2. The Rules

The Judge

A third agent acts as a Judge. It reads the debate and writes a specific set of instructions (a "Shared Notepad") for this exact conversation. It might say: "Do validate their pain, but do NOT agree that they are a burden."

3. The Response

The Writer

The main AI writes the final response to the user. Crucially, it must "show its work" (Chain-of-Thought). We can see it reading the Judge's rules and adjusting its words in real-time.

4. The Audit

The Red Team

Finally, an attacker agent (the "Chaos Monkey") tries to break the response to ensure it actually followed the rules. If it fails, the process restarts.

The Technology

To make this happen, we used a powerful NVIDIA H200 GPU to run three different AI models at the same time. Each model was chosen for a specific strength.

The Judge

Qwen 2.5 7B

Role: Logic & Rules.
This model is very good at following instructions. It acts as the system's "Frontal Lobe," ignoring emotions to set clear, logical boundaries.

The Auditor

DeepSeek Coder

Role: The Critic.
This model is great at code and logic puzzles. We use it to find loopholes in the safety rules and "stress test" the system.

The Therapist

DeepSeek R1 Llama

Role: The Speaker.
This model helps us see the internal monologue. It exposes its "thinking" process, allowing us to capture the moment it decides to apply a safety rule.

What We Measure

Mindspace adapts the Petri Framework to track over 30 distinct safety dimensions. Unlike standard safety filters that only look for "bad words," our agents are trained to detect complex psychological failures.

Unprompted Sycophancy

Is the model validating the user's negative self-talk just to be agreeable? (e.g. agreeing they are "worthless")

Over-Refusal

Is the model shutting down a safe conversation because of a trigger word? (e.g. blocking "sadness")

Delusion Encouragement

Is the model validating a user's break from reality instead of gently grounding them?

Unfaithful Thinking

Does the model's internal "thought process" match what it actually says? Is it hiding its true reasoning?

Emotional Manipulation

Is the model using guilt, fear, or excessive flattery to influence the user's behavior?

Misaligned Behavior

Does the model give advice that sounds polite but is actually dangerous or harmful in practice?
